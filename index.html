Geographic boundaries are important!

  • No boundaries, no data.
  • The way we draw boundaries can affect our understanding of the world (e.g., MAUP, Gerrymandering)


City and subcentre boundaries

  • Official city boundaries (e.g., CBSAs in the US, CMAs in Canada) do not exist for many countries (e.g. China, India, Korea)
    • Even when they exist, they are not always comparable across countries and over time.
  • Subcentre boundaries do not exist for any country, making it difficult to study subcentres.

Literature emerged on delineating clusters from granular data

  • City boundaries - Rozenfeld et al. (2011), Duranton (2015), Rappaport (2025)

  • Subcenters - McMillen (2001), Redfearn (2007)

  • Ethnic enclaves - Dai and Schiff (2023)

  • JUE special issue (2021) - de Bellefon et al, Dingle et al, Ch et al, Arribas-Bel et al, Baragwanath et al, Bosker et al, Moreno-Monroy et al, Galdo et al, Henderson et al, Dijkstra et al.

New empirical findings

US cities and subcentres

  • City size distribution follows lognormal distribution, rather than Zipf’s law.
  • The central place theory has a missing channel - land requirement
  • The spatial distribution of consumption across subcentres differs from that across cities. (e.g., drinking places vs. restaurants)

World cities from 1975 to 2020

  • World city size distribution follows lognormal distribution, rather than Zipf’s law.
  • Gibrat’s law holds for all cities except for the lower tail of the distribution.
  • Global population growth was driven primarily by the expansion of existing cities, rather than the creation of new ones.

Previous methods

Commuting-Flow-based approach

Group contiguous areas that have high level of interactions with an urban core, measured by commuting flows.

  • Official city boundaries: CBSAs, CMAs, …
  • Duranton (2015)

Challenge

  • Commuting flow data are relatively difficult to obtain.

Density based approaches

Set a density threshold on a smoothed density surface and find contiguous areas above the threshold.


Prevaling approach. Encompasses various methods:

  • grid-based approach
  • DBSCAN
  • city clustering algorithm

Main challenges

  • Need for local thresholds: Different areas often require different thresholds to separate clusters.
  • Arbitrary selection of density thresholds.

Not possible to separately identify all three clusters with a single threshold.

This can happen both within a city and across cities.

Need to choose the threshold parameters locally, using the “local knowledge”.

  • Different thresholds across areas make it difficult to compare the identified clusters.
  • Arbitrariness in cluster size and characteristics.

Core based approaches

Subcenter delineation: McMillen (2001), Redfearn (2007)

Start from local maxima and can identify all the peaks without adjusting any parameter

Challenge: Not trivial how to set the boundaries of each cluster on 2D surface.

Cities as Inverted Watersheds

Watershed - the area of land that flows to a common outlet (i.e., a pour point) and forms a basin.

Source: https://www.rcrcd.org/what-is-a-watershed

Efficient algorithms and tools available to identify watersheds from GIS raster data.

Mountains become watershed basins when inverted!

Works well on a 2D surface. Imagine placing a water drop on each pixel and let it flow down.

Given a smoothed density surface, the algorithm uniquely identifies all the peaks and boundaries. No parameters to adjust in this step.


The only parameter throughout the whole process is the smoothing parameter \(\sigma\) to create the smoothed density surface.

Adjusting \(\sigma\) allows us to delineate clusters at varying scales: larger values for entire cities and smaller values for subcenters within cities.

Two approaches to choosing the smoothing parameter

  • Statistics: find the single \(\sigma\) that best estimates the true density function.
  • Computer Vision: different features reveal themselves at different \(\sigma\). (e.g., leaves with low \(\sigma\) and tree with high \(\sigma\))

We follow the computer vision approach: subcenters with low \(\sigma\) and cities with high \(\sigma\).

The Structure of Images - Koenderink (1984)

Foundational work in computer vision


Causality in scale space

  • Gaussian smoothing is the only smoothing method that prevents the appearance of new peaks when smoothing.
  • As \(\sigma\) increases, the peaks can only merge.

These two results imply the hierarchical structure of features at different scales.


Our delineation preserves the topological hierarchy: a cluster at higher \(\sigma\) (e.g., cities) can be broken down into clusters at lower \(\sigma\) (e.g., subcenters).

Theoretical Grounding



  • A variation of the monocentric city model with three city centres at A, B, and C and workers choose where to live and work and how much land to consume.
  • Commuting flows follow the direction of increasing density.
  • The watershed boundaries coincide with the flow-based approach, but do not require commuting-flow data.

Summary

  • Monocentric city model. Produces similar results as the flow-based approach, without requiring commuting-flow data.
  • Use the same algorithm to identify clusters at different levels (e.g. entire cities or subcenters within cities) with a single parameter adjustment.
  • Topological hierarchy of clusters at different scales.
  • No need to handpick parameters for each region, allowing large-scale applications.
  • Applicable to various data types (e.g., point, polygon, raster), as long as we can generate a density surface.

Processing steps

Convert POI data to a raster data.

Project the POI data to USA Contiguous Albers Equal Area Conic, USGS (EPSG = 5070).

  • Preserve area size.
  • Use meters as distance unit.

Create a raster data with a 100 meter by 100 meter resolution, covering the whole continental US.

Count the number of POIs for each cell.

Smoothing the Surface

We apply the Gaussian filter to the raster data.

Two dimensional moving average with the Gaussian kernel as weights.

\[g(x,y) = \frac{1}{2 \pi \sigma^2} \exp^{-(x^2+y^2)/(2\sigma^2)}\]

x and y are the distances from the origin in the horizontal and the vertical axes.

We use 5 pixels (i.e., 500 meters) for \(\sigma\) as the baseline: the cells that are 500, 1000, and 1,500 meters away get 61%, 14%, and 1% weights compared to the own cell.

Invert the smoothed surface

Identify watersheds

For each pour point, identify the watershed cells flowing to the pour point using the D8 flow pointer.

Count the number of POIs for each watershed.

Delineating Whole Cities

Our method can identify a different level of clusters by choosing a different \(\sigma\).

Choosing \(\sigma\)

  • To find the optimal smoothing parameter \(\sigma\), we aim to align our delineated boundaries closely with CBSAs.
  • To quantify the similarity, we employ the City Jaccard (similarity) index \(J_C\)

\[J_C \equiv \frac{\sum_{k \in K} | U_k^{cbsa} \cap U_k^{ws}|}{| U^{cbsa} \cup U^{ws} |}\] where \(U_k^{cbsa}\) refers to CBSA \(k\) and \(U_k^{ws}\) denotes the largest cluster whose center is located in CBSA \(k\).

  • We can measure the size of a set \(|\cdot|\) using population or area size.

Optimal \(\sigma = 11.4km\)

Final output (Cities)

What business types tend to concentrate in larger subcenters (vs larger cities)?

Verifying this pattern at the subcenter level or in small cities has been difficult, due to the lack of consistent boundary data.

A potential missing channel in Central Place Theory: higher-order services may also require larger land areas.

New Fact - Urban Wage Premium for subcenters and cities

Urban Wage Premium: Average wages tend to rise with city size.

  • This relationship is well-documented and holds across many countries.

Natural questions to answer with the new data:

  • Does the Urban Wage Premium hold among subcenters within the same city?

  • Does it persist in smaller cities?

By applying areal interpolation to Census data, we estimate average income and population for each watershed cluster.

  • Areal interpolation is a spatial estimation method used to transfer data from one set of geographic boundaries (e.g., census tracts) to another (e.g., custom-defined clusters).

  • This allows us to estimate demographic and economic characteristics for non-standard spatial units such as our watershed clusters.

Urban Wage Premium for subcenters

  • Subcenter-level regression

\[log(Income_{i,j}) = \beta_1 \times log(Population_i) + \delta_j + \epsilon_{i,j}\] where \(Income_i\) refers to the average income of subcenter \(i\) in city \(j\)

  • City-level regression

\[log(Income_{j}) = \beta_1 \times log(Population_j) + \epsilon_j\] Note: Regressions are weighted by population sizes.

City Size Dynamics - Gibrat’s Law for all cities in the World

Eeckhout’s findings (2004) support Gibrat’s law for U.S. cities from 1990 to 2020, except for the bottom of the distribution. Our results are broadly consistent.

Zipf’s Law for all Cities in the World

Zipf’s Law states that a city’s size is inversely proportional to its rank.

\[S_r = \frac{S_1}{r}\] where \(S_r\) is the size of city with rank \(r\) and \(S_1\) is the size of the largest city.

–

Three ways to test Zipf’s Law:

  1. Run Zipf regression and check if Zipf’s coefficient \(-\beta_1\) is close to 1. \[log(Rank_i + 1) =\beta_0 + \beta_1 \times log(Population_i) + \epsilon_i\]

  2. Examine if city size distribution follows Pareto distribution with shape parameter 1.

  3. Zipf’s coefficient is stable across different minimum population cutoffs of the sample.

Conclusion

This paper proposes a new method to delineate clusters by utilizing the watershed segmentation algorithm.

  • Our method has theoretical background and produces similar outcomes as the flow-based methods, but does not require flow data.
  • We do not have to adjust parameter by location, allowing large scale applications.
  • We have only one global parameter \(\sigma\). We can use the same algorithm to identify the clusters of different levels, simply adjusting the parameter.
  • Our method can be applied to any type of spatial data as long as we can generate a density surface, such as pixel-based data (e.g. night-light satellite images) and polygon-based data (e.g., Census data)

By applying our algorithm to POI data and satelite-based population data, we delineate: - consumption subcenters in the continental US. First time in the literature. - cities in the world from 1975 to 2020.

These new datasets allow us to provide new empirical facts about urban structure and dynamics.
